# ManageEngine Content Style Guide — Claude Skill

## Purpose
Use this skill when writing SEO-optimized content pages for ManageEngine (ME). All content must adhere to ME style guidelines, sound humanized and natural, maintain proper grammar and punctuation, and contain zero plagiarism.

---

## Core Writing Principles

### Voice and Tone
- **Default POV:** Second person (recommended) for web/landing pages; third person for press releases and white papers.
- **Tone:** Neutral to casual for blogs and webpages; formal and factual for press releases and white papers.
- **Active voice:** Prefer active over passive. Scan for "was/were/by" constructions and rewrite.
  - Active: "The CEO delivered the presentation."
  - Passive (avoid): "The presentation was delivered by the CEO."
- **Contractions:** Use them to sound natural and friendly. Avoid in content destined for translation/international audiences.
- **No pop culture references:** Do not use references to sports, movies, TV shows, or celebrities to "spice up" content.
- **No AI-generated copy:** Content must be originally written. AI may assist with ideation, not drafting.

### Clarity and Consistency
- Use **active voice** as the default.
- Maintain **one point of view** throughout a page. Do not switch mid-paragraph (e.g., from "You can…" to "Organizations can…").
- Avoid **hyper-repetition**: if you repeat the same word multiple times in one sentence, rewrite using pronouns or synonyms.
- Choose one term and stick to it (e.g., don't alternate between "click" and "click on").
- **Inclusive language:** Replace biased terms:
  - blacklist → **blocklist**
  - whitelist → **allowlist**
  - master/slave → **primary/secondary**
  - man-in-the-middle → **manipulator-in-the-middle**
  - man-hours → **work hours**
  - hallucination (AI) → **distortion** or **creative gap-filling**
- Use preferred pronouns (they/them, she/her, he/him). Never assume gender.
- Avoid terms like "ninja," "rockstar," "wizard," "Jedi," or "superhero" for people.
- **No emojis** in formal communications (text-based content). Emojis are only used in social media posts.

## SEO Content Guidelines

### Webpage / Landing Pages
- **Casing:** Sentence case throughout. No period at the end of headings (question marks are fine).
- **Meta titles:** Title case — capitalize any word longer than three letters.
- **CTAs:** Sentence case. Keep CTAs above the fold when possible.
- **Bullet lists:** Use an introductory phrase or sentence before a list.
- **Relative time:** Avoid "last month," "a week ago." Use fixed dates like "late 2023" or "January 2024."
- **Two page types:**
  - *Landing pages* — campaign-based, can be more casual, aimed at prospects.
  - *Feature pages* — technical, aimed at current users.

### Blog Posts
- **Length:** 300–700 words typical; 500 words average.
- **Titles:** Longer titles perform better. Sentence case. No period.
- **Structure:** Begin with an intro paragraph — never start with a subheading directly under the title.
- **Lists:** Always include an introductory phrase/sentence before a bullet or numbered list; never place a list directly under a subheading.
- **CTA:** Include at the end of the blog.
- **Series titles format:**
  - "[Series title]—Part [N]" or
  - "[Series title], Part [N]: [Entry title]" — Series title in sentence case, "Part" capitalized, entry title in sentence case.

### Headlines and Headings
- **Sentence case** for most content types (blogs, articles, e-books, webpages, newsletters, case studies, white papers).
- **Title case** for press releases and meta titles.
- No terminal punctuation on section heads (except question marks).
- Use numerals in headlines.
- Avoid hyphens in titles/headings; use a colon or pipe (|) as a separator instead.
  - Never: "Web traffic monitoring - Site24x7"
  - Use: "Web traffic monitoring: Site24x7" or "Web traffic monitoring | Site24x7"

### Sourcing and Accuracy
- Every stat or metric **must be sourced**. Prefer hyperlinks. If not possible, include full vendor name and report title.
- Sources should ideally be 1–2 years old. Sources 3–4 years old: use with judgment. Avoid sources older than 4 years.
- Do not source from direct competitors.
- Verify that quoted metrics match what the source actually says.

## Grammar Rules

### Numbers
- **Headlines/titles/headers:** Always use numerals.
- **Body copy:** Spell out zero through nine; use numerals for 10 and above.
- Numbers that start a sentence: always spell out.
- Numbers over three digits: use commas (e.g., 150,000).
- Big numbers: write in words (e.g., "three million"). Abbreviate only when space is limited (e.g., 1k, 150k).
- **Ordinals:** Spell out first through ninth; use numerals for 10th and above (same for headlines).
- **Percentages:** Always use numerals followed by %. Spell out only when a percentage starts a sentence (exception: okay to use numerals in bullet lists).
- **Fractions:** Spell out (e.g., "two-thirds"). Use decimals when a fraction can't be easily written out (e.g., 1.375).
- **Decimals:** Use a decimal point for non-fraction numbers.

### Articles
- Use **a/an** for singular first-mention nouns. Use **the** for known/previously mentioned items.
- **an** before vowel sounds; **a** before consonants.
- Mass nouns (e.g., "software") take no article: "ServiceDesk Plus is help desk software" ✓ — not "a help desk software."
- Initialisms (each letter pronounced separately) take "the": "the GDPR," "the FBI." Acronyms (pronounced as a word) do not: "HIPAA compliance."

### Subject-Verb Agreement
- Singular subjects take singular verbs; plural subjects take plural verbs.
- **Data** is a mass noun — always use singular: "data is," not "data are."
- **Criteria** is plural; **criterion** is singular.

### Contractions
- Use freely in casual/blog content for natural tone.
- Avoid in content that will be translated.
- **It's vs. its:** "It's" = "it is/it has." "Its" = possessive.

### Pronouns
- Use they/them/their as a singular gender-neutral pronoun. "One" is not acceptable as a pronoun.
- Reflexive singular: "themself" is acceptable.

### Point of View Consistency
- Do not shift POV within a sentence or paragraph.
- Consistency across the whole page is expected.

## Punctuation Rules

### Commas
- Use the **Oxford (serial) comma**: "rice, beans, and cheese" — always include the comma before "and" or "or" in a list.
- Separate two independent clauses: "Zoho is a software company, and it has over 2,500 employees."
- Set off appositives with commas.
- Separate coordinate adjectives.
- Use in numbers of four or more digits: 2,500.

### Colons
- Introduce lists or emphasize statements.
- If a complete sentence follows the colon, capitalize the first word.
- If not a complete sentence, do not capitalize.

### Periods
- Always inside quotation marks.
- One period only — never two in a row (e.g., "voltage, etc." not "voltage, etc..")
- Inside parentheses only when the parenthetical is a complete sentence; outside when it is part of the main sentence.

### Dashes
- **Hyphen (-):** Compound words and adjectives (e.g., "end-user activity").
- **Em dash (—):** Abrupt change in thought or emphatic aside — no spaces on either end. Use sparingly. Also used for quote attribution: "Quote." —Name, Title.
- **En dash (–):** Ranges and spans (e.g., "Aug. 10–20," "20–30 days").
- In titles/headings: never use a hyphen as a separator. Use a colon or pipe.

### Exclamation Points
- Use sparingly — maximum 1–2 in an entire piece.
- Never use more than one at a time.
- Goes inside quotation marks.

### Question Marks
- Inside quotation marks generally; outside when questioning a quoted statement.

### Quotation Marks
- Double quotes for direct speech and article titles.
- Single quotes only for a quote within a quote.
- Commas, exclamation points, and question marks go inside the closing quote mark.
- Colons and semicolons go outside the closing quote mark.

### Apostrophes
- Possessive not ending in -s: add 's (e.g., "the ship's sail").
- Possessive ending in -s: add 's unless next word starts with s (e.g., "the hostess's invitation," "the hostess' seat").
- Possessive proper noun ending in -s: add only an apostrophe (e.g., "ServiceDesk Plus' UI").

### Parentheses
- Use sparingly for inessential information.
- No extra spacing inside parentheses.

### Ellipses
- Three dots only. Use sparingly — not for drama or titles.
- In presentations: ellipses at end of first slide only when a sentence is split across slides.

### Ampersands
- Use only in event titles and brand names. Write "and" everywhere else.

### Semicolons
- Separate closely related independent clauses.
- Always outside the closing quotation mark.

## Capitalization Rules

### Title Case vs. Sentence Case

| Content type | Casing |
|---|---|
| Blog titles and headings | Sentence case, no period |
| Webpage titles and headings | Sentence case, no period |
| Meta titles | Title case, no period |
| Press release titles/headings | Title case, no period |
| Email subject lines | Sentence case, no period |
| Article titles and headings | Sentence case, no period |
| E-book, white paper, case study titles | Sentence case, no period |
| Social media copy | Sentence case |
| Infographics, tables, solution briefs | Sentence case, no period |

**Title case rule:** Capitalize every word except articles (a, an, the), conjunctions (and, but, or), and prepositions (on, in) — unless they're longer than three letters or begin the sentence/title.

**Sentence case rule:** Capitalize the first word, proper nouns, and product names only.

### Hyphenated Words in Titles
- Title case: capitalize the word after the hyphen ("Out-of-Sequence").
- Do not capitalize the second part of a compound word: "Workflow" not "WorkFlow."

### General Capitalization
- Spell out all caps emphasis — use **bold** or *italics* instead.
- URL and email addresses: all lowercase.
- Product edition types: capitalize (Professional, Standard, Enterprise, Free edition).
- Titles preceding names: title case ("Director of Operations Jane Doe"). Titles following names: lowercase ("Jane Doe, director of operations").
- Department names: lowercase when general; title case for specific references ("the Finance Department at Zylker").

- Refer to companies/products as "it," not "they."
- **Microsoft**: always spell out; never "MS."
- Rebranded products: use both names for first year (e.g., "Microsoft Entra ID, previously Azure AD"), then just new name.

## Formatting and Style Specifics

### Dates
- Default (US): "Saturday, Jan. 24" or "Saturday, Jan. 24, 2024"
- Month abbreviations: Jan., Feb., Mar., Apr., May, June, July, Aug., Sept., Oct., Nov., Dec.
- Regional (D-M-Y): "24 Jan. 2024"
- Time: numerals with am/pm — no space, no minutes for on-the-hour times: "7am," "7:30pm"
- En dash for ranges: "Aug. 10–20, 2023"

### Money
- Currency symbol before amount: "$20," "€20 million," "¥500"
- Include cents only if nonzero: "$20" or "$19.99"
- For international context: "USD 22.67 billion" — don't combine symbol and code.

### Units (tech metrics)
- No space between number and unit: 25GB, 2.4GHz, 1Mbps, 125MB

### URLs and Phone Numbers
- URLs: sentence case, drop "http://www."
- Phone: use periods, no parentheses/dashes: "555.867.5309" or "+1.404.123.4567"

### Tables
- Sentence case across all headings and cells.
- Column headers: bold, center-aligned, 1pt larger than body font.
- Cells with sentences/fragments: left-aligned (leftmost column) or center-aligned.
- Single words/phrases: center-aligned. Numbers: center-aligned. Bullet lists: left-aligned.

### File Extensions
- General reference: all caps without period (PDF, GIF, HTML, JPGs).
- Specific file names: lowercase.

### Figures (images in articles/blogs)
- Caption format: "Figure N. Description." (e.g., "Figure 1. Dashboard overview.")
- Direct reference in text: "As can be seen in Figure 3, the settings are modifiable."
- Indirect reference: "(Fig. 3)"

### Text Emphasis
- Use **bold** or *italics* for emphasis — never all caps, never underline in body copy.
- Use italics for titles of works (books, movies) and error messages in help docs.
- Bold a colon when the preceding text is bold: **Feature name:** Description.
- The period/punctuation at the end of a bold phrase is NOT bold.

## Special Topic Guidelines

### Writing About the Cloud
- Data is saved "in the cloud" — not "on the cloud."
- Applications run "in the cloud" — not "on the cloud."

### Writing About Cyberattacks and Ransomware
- Report facts. Do not advise what the victim "should have done."
- Structure: (1) Report the attack, (2) categorize attack type, (3) discuss general defense approaches (product-agnostic), (4) introduce ManageEngine product that helps.

### Writing About Other Companies
- Honor the company's own capitalization and naming conventions.
- Always check official websites for correct product/brand names.
- Refer to companies as "it," not "they."

### Writing About AI
- Zia (Zoho's chatbot): it/its
- "AI hallucination" → use **distortion** or **creative gap-filling** instead.
- "agentic AI" is the correct term.

### Names and People
- First mention: full name (first and last). Subsequent mentions: last name only.
- For hypothetical/fictional characters: first name only.
- Casual copy: first name after first full-name mention is acceptable.

### Customer Quotes
- May be edited for readability without changing meaning or tone.
- Use square brackets for word-choice changes: [updated word].
- If a quote is very poor quality, paraphrase rather than quote directly.
- Full name and designation on first mention; last name thereafter. Always full name when quote is in a standalone text box.

### Trademark Usage
- Gartner®: trademark on first mention; on all instances of report names.
- ITIL®: trademark on first mention. Drop thereafter.
- MITRE ATT&CK®: trademark on first mention. Drop thereafter in body. Never modify the trademark.
- For emails: include trademark on first instance in both subject line and body.

## Plagiarism and Originality

- All content must be original. Do not copy or closely paraphrase external sources.
- Every sourced stat or fact must be linked or attributed.
- Do not source from direct competitors.
- Verify that quoted metrics match the original source exactly.
- Sources should be no older than 1–2 years (max 4 years depending on the stat type).
